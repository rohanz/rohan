## the temptation of tantalus

If you've ever spent enough time around 18-25 year old finance hopefuls you might find yourself momentarily believing you're rubbing shoulders with a couple of the next <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Charlie Munger, Warren Buffett's right hand at Berkshire Hathaway for six decades and the sharper tongue of the pair.">Mungers</span> and <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Warren Buffett, the most famous investor alive and finance-hopeful shorthand for 'made billions by being right about companies'.">Buffetts</span>. I've sat through a lot of these conversations: overnight crypto pumps, high frequency options trading, some strange techniques returning 20x that nobody could quite explain. The stories of seemingly boundless reward were <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="From Tantalus of Greek myth: condemned to stand beneath fruit that pulled away whenever he reached for it. Eternally close, never his. Keep that in mind, it's pretty much the story of this whole article.">tantalising</span>, in the original sense. Luscious fruit hung just overhead, begging to be grabbed. I was itching to put my own money on the line and take a shot at it. Some of them even had <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Running a trading strategy against historical price data to see how it would have performed.">backtests</span> to back them up, and that's gotta be surefire proof that it's legit, right?

...Right?

That's roughly how I got into testing strategies, and, spoiler alert, how I was disillusioned almost immediately. A large chunk of backtests people flaunt are a little less than honest, and oftentimes they don't even know it. Information leaks in from the future, costs get ignored, and the dataset conveniently forgets every company that went broke. As it turns out, a large part of professional quant research boils down to hunting these lies down in your own work before believing anything. Advanced cynicism, wrapped in maths, which sounded like a fun endeavour to dip my toes into.

I could've tested strategies on an existing platform, but existing frameworks have already made all the decisions where the mistakes, and therefore the learnings, lie: when <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="A fill is your order actually executing: at what moment, and at what price. Backtests live or die on this assumption.">fills</span> happen, when costs count, how the data gets split. I would've inherited their answers and none of the thought reflexes. I can't audit a black box I didn't build, which is a problem when the black box is the thing under investigation.

So I built all of it myself: this is the strategy-research arm of <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="My trading-firm-in-miniature monorepo. This article covers the strategy research; a separate article covers the execution systems (matching engine, risk controls, live paper trading), and a third a fine-tuned AI analyst.">quantlab</span>, my miniature trading firm. Through the process, I hit just about every pitfall you can imagine, and many of those inform the article you're about to read.

## the engine, and the first lesson

The heart of any of this is the backtesting engine: the simulator that replays market history one day at a time, hands each day to a strategy, executes whatever trades it asks for, and tracks what the portfolio would have been worth. Every result in this article comes out of it, so if it lies, everything downstream lies with it. That's why I built it first, and why it got the paranoia.

Before any strategy, though, data. I took ten years of daily prices for about 500 current <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="The S&P 500: an index of roughly 500 of the largest US-listed companies. 'Names' is trader slang for the individual stocks.">S&P names</span> plus benchmark <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Exchange-traded funds: baskets of many stocks that trade like a single stock. Useful as one-ticker stand-ins for a whole market.">ETFs</span>, pulled from free sources into <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="A fast columnar file format. The whole dataset is regenerated by one script and never hand-edited.">Parquet</span> files. A quick validation layer rejected about 19 tickers outright for broken histories. One of the checks I implemented ("no sane stock moves 50% in a day") falsely rejected PG&E during its 2019 bankruptcy, which was a pretty quick reminder that data cleaning rules encode assumptions about what markets are allowed to do.

The engine itself started as the 'simplest correct' thing, so to speak: two hundred lines that read prices, walk forward one day at a time, and track cash and positions. The only real design rule it had was to make <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Using information in a simulation that wouldn't have existed at that moment in real trading, like trading at today's close using today's close. The most common way backtests lie.">lookahead bias</span> structurally impossible, rather than just discouraged. Strategies are handed only the price history up to yesterday, as a sliced array. Signals computed on day T trade at day T+1's open. In plain terms: if the strategy decides to buy after seeing Tuesday's close, the earliest a real trader could act is Wednesday morning, so that's when the engine fills it. Trading Tuesday's decision at Tuesday's price means using a number that didn't exist yet when the decision was made, and that single-day slip is enough to make a losing strategy look brilliant. I threw in a deliberate cheat strategy in the test suite, too, and the test asserts that the cheat is *unwritable*, not just unprofitable. <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Commissions plus slippage: the gap between paper prices and what you actually pay to trade. Modelled here at 6 basis points (0.06%) per side.">Transaction costs</span> are always on.

The first strategy through the engine was a simple <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Buy when the average price of the last 50 days rises above the average of the last 200 days; sell when it falls below. A classic trend-following rule.">moving average crossover</span> on single names like <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Apple's stock ticker symbol. Tickers are the short codes stocks trade under.">AAPL</span> and <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="The ticker of the biggest ETF tracking the S&P 500 index. It works as a stand-in for 'the US stock market'.">SPY</span>. It lost to just buying and holding (on Apple, over the decade: +332% vs +1,197%), and it barely paid any costs at 11 trades in a decade. Cost drag scales with <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="How much of the portfolio gets bought and sold over a period. High turnover means paying transaction costs over and over.">turnover</span>, a fact that'll ruin a couple of strategies later in this article.

## pairs trading: a DSP student meets a regime change

With an engine I could finally trust, I needed a strategy actually worth running through it, and a moving average crossover wasn't going to cut it. I started with the classic <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Strategies that profit from statistical relationships between securities rather than from betting on market direction.">statistical arbitrage</span> play: find two stocks whose prices historically move together (<span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Two price series are cointegrated when some fixed combination of them is mean-reverting: they wander, but the gap between them keeps coming home.">cointegrated</span>), then trade the gap when it stretches, betting it closes. Mechanically, the strategy watches the <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="The gap between the two stocks' prices after applying the hedge ratio. When the pair is behaving, it wobbles around zero.">spread</span> between the two stocks and bets when it stretches more than two <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="A measure of how unusual today's gap is compared to its normal wobble. A z-score of 2 means the spread is stretched about twice as far as usual.">standard deviations</span>, exiting when it comes home. A scan across about 190 candidates surfaced pairs like Google/Home Depot, Microsoft/Walmart, and J&J/UnitedHealth. Tested fairly (pairs selected on 2016–2020 data, traded only on 2021 onward), the textbook version failed, badly. **The Google/Home Depot pair lost 41.1%**, Microsoft/Walmart lost 16.4%. In practice, the relationships drift, so a fixed <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="How many dollars of stock B to hold against each dollar of stock A so the paired position isn't just a bet on the market going up.">hedge ratio</span> goes stale, and recalculating and refitting it daily churns fees.

The textbook version re-fits the hedge ratio from a rolling 250 day window every single day, and that daily re-fitting generated roughly 1,400 trades in five years, paying costs on every single adjustment. The losses weren't only costs, though: the signal itself decays. Cointegration isn't an immutable, unbreakable law, but more of a snapshot of two businesses that happened to be pulled by the same forces for a while. Google and Home Depot moved together through 2016–2020; then the pandemic re-priced e-commerce and housing on completely different logic, and the "stable" relationship my scan had certified simply stopped existing. The strategy kept betting the gap would come home, but it never did.

As a bit of a signal processing nerd (colossal understatement, see also: <a href="/projects/bqst">bqst</a>, my DSP project) I realised all I needed was a method of filtering out high frequency noise while accounting for gradual drift. A <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="A recursive estimator that tracks a hidden, slowly-changing quantity through noisy observations. Used everywhere from GPS to radar; here it tracks the drifting hedge ratio between two stocks.">Kalman filter</span> was the obvious answer. A Kalman filter treats the hedge ratio as a slowly drifting quantity. Each day the filter predicts where the spread should be and how confident it is in that prediction. When the actual price arrives, the surprise (how far reality landed from the prediction, measured in units of the filter's own uncertainty) is *exactly* the stretched-spread signal the strategy wants. The <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="How many 'normal wobbles' the spread is away from where it should be. A z-score of 2 means stretched about twice as far as usual; the strategy bets at 2 and exits near 0.">z-score</span> falls out of the filter for free: no lookback window, no separate estimation step, no extra parameters to overfit. And position weights freeze at entry, so the strategy stops paying the daily re-adjustment tax. On my first calibration it turned Google/Home Depot's -41% into roughly breakeven and cut <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="The worst peak-to-trough loss along the way. A strategy that ends up flat but was down 50% in the middle still had a -50% drawdown.">drawdowns</span> three to six times over.

It's around here that I made the exact mistake I'd built the engine to prevent. That first calibration of the filter's two tuning knobs had faintly peeked at the test window. Retuning them using *only* the selection window cost the Google/Home Depot pair 20 points and flipped its sign, from roughly breakeven to -19.4% net. The true headline is -41% into -19%: still a loss, but half the bleeding. Across all three pairs the truthful filter still beat the static version comfortably (average net +2.3% versus -19.3%), so the adaptivity genuinely helped. But cash-like returns on the good pairs with a losing one in the mix means daily-bar pairs trading on free data just isn't profitable.

## factors and survivorship bias

Pairs trading bets on one relationship at a time, and I'd just watched those relationships die. The other end of the spectrum is to stop caring about any single stock and trade the whole market at once: rank all 500 names by some signal, buy the top slice, re-rank every month. Strategies built that way are called factor strategies, and they meant some big engineering decisions first. The daily event engine assumes cleanly aligned histories, and a 500-name universe is ragged with IPOs and gaps. Rather than generalise the engine before it needed it, I ran the factor tests as a separate monthly pipeline: form the signal at month-end, earn the *following* month's return. The lookahead rule survives the format change by construction.

Then I ran the classic <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Systematic return patterns documented across decades, like momentum (recent winners keep winning for a while) or low-volatility (calmer stocks do better than they should).">factor strategies</span> as a matched pair: <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Rank all stocks by their return over the past 12 months (skipping the most recent month, which tends to reverse), buy the top 10%, re-rank monthly.">12-1 momentum</span> (buy the past year's winners) and its defensive opposite, <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Buy the calmest stocks, those with the lowest recent day-to-day volatility. Historically they have beaten what their low risk would predict.">low-volatility</span> (buy the calmest names). Momentum backtested at +840% over nine years; low-vol at +208%, trailing as a defensive factor should, especially over a decade-long bull run.

The +840% is the one that stands out, of course. Maybe those finance hopefuls I was talking about at the start were onto something. That's the type of number I could have led this article with, but also exactly the kind of number you should treat with a healthy dose of scepticism. The universe is *today's* S&P members, so companies that failed along the way have vanished from the data. To investigate, I measured the damage by benchmarking my survivors against <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="RSP, an ETF that holds every S&P 500 stock in equal amounts. Because it held the losers while they fell out of the index, it reflects reality without survivorship bias.">the real equal-weight S&P ETF</span> that held the losers as they died: **+4.6% per year of pure <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="The inflation in results that comes from testing only on assets that survived to today. The dead ones took their losses out of your dataset.">survivorship bias</span>**, persistent in 94 of 125 months, and worst exactly where it flatters momentum.

Momentum, specifically, gets flattered: momentum buys recent winners, and a survivors-only universe is *made of* winners: every stock that cratered and got delisted has been removed from the list of things the strategy could have bought. So the bias doesn't inflate all strategies equally; it most inflates the strategy that chases the survivors' defining trait.

### the ML layer that lost

Then the machine learning layer. The dataset here is a few hundred monthly <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="One date's snapshot of every stock in the universe at once. A decade of monthly snapshots is only about 120 of them, so the dataset is far smaller than it sounds.">cross-sections</span> of noisy, <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="The statistical rules keep changing over time: relationships that held in 2016 need not hold in 2022. Most ML assumes they don't change.">non-stationary</span> tabular data, which puts it firmly in <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="An ML method (here LightGBM) that combines many small decision trees into a predictor. The workhorse for tabular data like this.">gradient-boosted-trees</span> territory; a neural net on data this small would mostly be a more expensive way to overfit. So: gradient boosting over five price-based features (momentum at two horizons, last month's reversal, volatility, and distance from the 52-week high), trained to predict which stocks outrank others next month. Predicting what a stock will return next month is close to hopeless, so I didn't even try. Predicting that stock A probably beats stock B is a gentler target, and a portfolio only needs the ordering anyway.

Again, I evaluated it the strict way: <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Train only on data before year Y, leave a gap so no information leaks across the boundary, then predict year Y. Repeat, walking forward. The right way to evaluate ML on time series.">walk-forward with an embargo</span>, costs included. Ordinary cross-validation shuffles time, letting the model train on the future it'd later be tested on. Walk-forward means the model only ever predicts years it hasn't seen, with a gap so nothing bleeds across the boundary.

The result was **+325%, versus plain momentum's +446% over the same 2020+ window** (that's the full-period +840% shrunk to the shorter walk-forward window, not a contradiction), and it wasn't even a smoother ride for the trouble: Sharpe 0.96 against momentum's 1.24. The feature importances came back nearly flat, around 0.20 each; the model found no hierarchy among the five signals, it just blended correlated things into mush, diluting the strongest one and adding turnover. Annoyingly, that's exactly what the literature warned me would happen. I kept the negative result as the centerpiece: *The ML layer, with critical evaluation, lost to the boring baseline.* If nothing else, it's a result I can defend, which is more than I can say for +840%.

## mining the community archives

These factors were alright, but it wasn't close to the mythical success stories I'd heard. Luckily for me, the internet is full of famous strategies with spectacular backtests. <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="A community quantitative-trading platform that ran from 2011 to 2020, where thousands of people shared and backtested strategies. Its archives outlived it.">Quantopian</span> left behind an archive of them. I picked three of the most-cited and tested them with the same scepticism.

**First, the famous one.** Peter Guenther's "In & Out" was the most-hyped strategy of Quantopian's final year: hold equities normally, but flee to bonds when a basket of proxy ETFs (industrial metals, the dollar, the gold-to-silver ratio) flashes early signs of stress. Its published backtest showed roughly 1,700% since 2008. Now these were the numbers I'd been trying to get at. On my 2016-to-2026 data it returned 306% with a Sharpe of 0.85, while simply holding the <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="QQQ, the ETF tracking the 100 largest non-financial Nasdaq companies. A common equity benchmark for tech-tilted strategies.">Nasdaq-100</span> returned 600% at 0.94. It shaved three points off the worst drawdown (-32% versus -35%) but gave up half the return to do it, parked in bonds 20% of the time and flitting in and out. The famous number was practically all derived from the 2008 crash, which my data didn't reach. It was effectively a worse buy-and-hold with a nervous disposition. Despite all that, I'm <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Running a strategy against live market prices with simulated money: real conditions, no real losses.">paper trading</span> it anyway, clearly labelled, to watch it underperform in real time.

**The only one I was actually hopeful for.** Scaling momentum to a constant volatility target is one of the few momentum improvements most academic literature says survives out of sample, by dodging the sharp reversals that make momentum dangerous. I <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Writing down the predicted outcome before running the test, so the result can't be rationalised after the fact.">pre-registered</span> that it would improve risk-adjusted return. It didn't. It had the same -20% drawdown, a lower <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Return earned per unit of risk taken. Higher is better; it rewards smooth gains over lucky volatile ones.">Sharpe</span> (1.02 versus 1.24), and barely a third of the return (160% versus 446%). Volatility targeting protects against momentum crashes, and 2016-to-2026 never handed this portfolio one. Scaling into cash during loud months only forfeited upside for insurance that never ended up being claimed anyway.

**The one that reused my other project.** Chris Cain's "Quality Companies in an Uptrend" combines a quality score (profitability, low debt) with momentum and a <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="A switch that only allows buying when the broad market is trending up, moving to cash or bonds otherwise.">market-regime filter</span>. Building it let me wire this project into the <a href="/projects/quantlab-analyst">finetuned AI analyst</a>: the quality scores come from the same SEC fundamentals that project extracts, under a strict <span class="gloss-term" data-gloss="Using only information that was actually public at each point in the past. A company's annual figures aren't used until the date its report would have been filed, roughly three months after the fiscal year ends.">point-in-time</span> discipline so the backtest never reads a number before it would have been published. It returned 344% against plain momentum's 840%, with a worse drawdown (-28% versus -20%) and a worse Sharpe (0.86 versus 1.22): the regime filter dragged in a decade that mostly went up, and actually worsened the drawdown by whipsawing, exiting after drops and re-entering after recoveries. The quality tilt gave up momentum in a growth-led market.

## the fate of tantalus

The three famous strategies only dug up three unimpressive negatives, with one thread running through all of them. On a survivorship-inflated bull decade, every filter, gate, and clever overlay is just drag. Plain momentum is stubbornly, boringly, and infuriatingly hard to beat. Every spectacular number pulled away the moment I reached for it with a fair test. 

I started this whole project with a small, naive hope that I'd leave with a golden ticket. Maybe I'd find one of those unusual strategies those finance hopefuls talked about, and I'd never have to work a day for the rest of my life.

I'm still writing these portfolio articles, so you know how that went.

## important lessons, disappointing truths

1. **Discipline is the product.** Not the strategies, the reflexes. Lookahead made unwritable rather than discouraged, costs always on, and biases measured in numbers someone can argue with (+4.6%/yr).
2. **Adaptivity beats refitting.** The Kalman filter was a DSP-to-finance transfer that turned -41% into -19% (still a loss, but half the bleeding), and the tuning leak I caught in my own work cost 20 points when confessed. That gives you a pretty good indication of how much the lies are worth.
3. **Boring baselines are extremely hard to beat.** An ML layer, three famous community strategies, and a stack of clever overlays all lost to plain momentum once tested fairly. Nobody dangles *that* result in front of you.
